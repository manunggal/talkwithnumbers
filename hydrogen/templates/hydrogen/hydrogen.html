Understanding the Magnitude of Climate Change and Clean Energy

Climate change represents one of the most significant challenges facing our planet. It's a "huge" issue, not just in scale but in complexity. The transition to clean energy is pivotal in our efforts to address this global concern. However, it's essential to approach this transition thoughtfully, recognizing the diverse viewpoints and concerns that exist.

The Scale of Hydrogen Production and Its Impacts

In 2021, our world produced an enormous 94 million tonnes of hydrogen. Surprisingly, about 95% of this was derived from fossil fuels. Notably, approximately 40% of this hydrogen is used in oil refineries - industries directly linked to the fossil fuel economy we aim to transition away from. To avoid catastrophic climate change, a significant shift in this sector is crucial. Even if we eliminate the portion of hydrogen used in oil refining, a staggering 56-60 million tonnes per year would still be needed for various industries. This number assumes no growth in hydrogen demand, which might not be the case as industries evolve.

Green Hydrogen: A Path to Decarbonization & The Clean Hydrogen Ladder Concept

Assuming we can produce sufficient affordable green hydrogen through electrolysis, the focus should shift to decarbonizing hydrogen feedstocks for diverse industries. This strategy is key to reducing our overall carbon footprint without disrupting industrial processes that are vital for our economy. Given these dynamics, it might be premature to heavily invest in hydrogen as a fuel for transportation. While it's an exciting area of development, our immediate focus should be on areas where green hydrogen can replace its carbon-intensive counterparts most effectively. Michael Liebreich's Clean Hydrogen Ladder concept offers a clear visualization of this issue.


The Hydrogen Ladder, Version 5.0

It emphasizes the need to prioritize the application of green hydrogen where it is most effective and necessary, underscoring that not all uses of hydrogen are equally beneficial in the context of decarbonization.
